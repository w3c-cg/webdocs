### Privacy Primer discussion

- Glossed version of the W3C Privacy Principles (https://www.w3.org/TR/privacy-principles/)
- With a focus on Web developers
- Not meant to be as academic like the Privacy Principles spec.
- Got the first two sections for this: Working with user data as well as user recognition and tracking
- Would be good to have review by Dan on this plan.
- Recognition is the act of observing how requests belong together, so there are legitimate and illegitimate uses.
- Related concepts: single site tracking (usually legitimate), cross site tracking (usually illegitimate)
- What does the user expect to happen with their identity? If they don't know it is happening or they don't agree, then it is not okay.
- Sanctioned and unsanctioned tracking: Cookies are a visible way of doing tracking. Researchers and regulators etc can see what is going on.
- Fingerprinting and pixel tracking on the other hand is completely opaque, so that falls under illegitimate tracking.
- See the outline which is quite detailed now and we can turn this into a document.
- The outline now has empty pages, each containing a heading and the more detailed TOC to be filled in.
- Tracking: https://wiki.mozilla.org/Security/Anti_tracking_policy, so here Mozilla calls it "Tracking via unintended identification techniques"
- So different wordings for the same idea and we can clear all that up. Same with the article Jon sent last time https://webkit.org/tracking-prevention-policy/
- Next article can be on tracking then.
- There is also a thing called "Privacy labor" where users are asked to read long terms of services to figure out what is going on.
- Our own project, the BCD collector, for example, has a short and precise privacy notice, https://collector.openwebdocs.org
